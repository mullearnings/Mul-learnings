The Sacred Text — Untouched

ਸੋਚੈ ਸੋਚਿ ਨ ਹੋਵਈ ਜੇ ਸੋਚੀ ਲਖ ਵਾਰ ॥
ਚੁਪੈ ਚੁਪ ਨ ਹੋਵਈ ਜੇ ਲਾਇ ਰਹਾ ਲਿਵ ਤਾਰ ॥
ਭੁਖਿਆ ਭੁਖ ਨ ਉਤਰੀ ਜੇ ਬੰਨਾ ਪੁਰੀਆ ਭਾਰ ॥
ਸਹਸ ਸਿਆਣਪਾ ਲਖ ਹੋਹਿ ਤ ਇਕ ਨ ਚਲੈ ਨਾਲਿ ॥

Bhai Vir Singh — Arth & Vyākhyā

A faithful translation of Bhai Vir Singh Ji's Steek. Key Punjabi terms are kept where they carry meaning that English cannot fully capture, and explained on first use. These are his words and his interpretation.

Prākathan — Preface Having recited the Salok of "Aad Sach," Guru Ji now recites the first Pauri. In the Mool Mantar, attainment of Akaal Purakh was said to come through the Guru. Guru Ji now explains the means by which that attainment may actually be achieved. First, those methods are described which were tried and proved unsuccessful. Then the successful method is revealed.
ਸੋਚੈ ਸੋਚਿ ਨ ਹੋਵਈ ਜੇ ਸੋਚੀ ਲਖ ਵਾਰ ॥
Arth

To attain the Sat Saroop (the True Form), the kooR dī pāl — the wall of falsehood — that stands between us and Him must be broken. That wall cannot be broken by bodily acts. Performing soch — ritual purification and cleansing of the body — does not produce soch of the man (mind). Even if one were to purify oneself a hundred thousand times, the inner wall remains untouched.

Note: Bhai Vir Singh points to a deliberate double meaning in the word ਸੋਚੈ. In one reading it means ritual cleansing of the body (soch). In the other it means thinking and reflection (soch-veechaar). Both meanings apply — and both fail equally, because both are functions of the man (mind) and intellect, which cannot reach the Truth on their own.
ਚੁਪੈ ਚੁਪ ਨ ਹੋਵਈ ਜੇ ਲਾਇ ਰਹਾ ਲਿਵ ਤਾਰ ॥
Arth

One might reason: if I simply stop speaking, then falsehood is stopped — I have automatically become sachiaar (one who has become truth itself). To this, Guru Ji responds: keeping outwardly silent — observing moun (the vow of silence) — cannot produce the inner chup (stillness, peace) of the man, which is driven by the restless pull of vashnaa (desire and craving). Even if one maintains an unbroken, continuous liv — an inward focus — the surface quiet does not reach the depths.

Moun is a form of tap (austerity). Like the heron standing motionless at the water's edge — it appears completely still and absorbed, but inside its mind is entirely focused on catching fish. The silence is an act, not a state. The haumai (ego — the deeply rooted sense of "I") does not become quiet just because the mouth does.
ਭੁਖਿਆ ਭੁਖ ਨ ਉਤਰੀ ਜੇ ਬੰਨਾ ਪੁਰੀਆ ਭਾਰ ॥
Arth

One might then reason: if I observe brat (fasting and self-denial), surely I will become sachiaar. To this, Guru Ji says: by remaining hungry through fasting, the trishnaa (craving — the deep inner hunger that dwells within desires) is never satisfied. Even if one were to receive, as a reward for fasting, the wealth and abundance of all fourteen realms, the trishnaa would only grow. It is the nature of trishnaa that the more it receives, the more it wants.

Trishnaa is not hunger of the stomach — it is hunger of the ego. It arises directly from haumai. As long as haumai remains, trishnaa remains. The scriptural reward promised for fasting is worldly attainment — but worldly attainment feeds trishnaa, it does not end it. The kooR dī pāl (the wall of falsehood, which is haumai) stays standing.
ਸਹਸ ਸਿਆਣਪਾ ਲਖ ਹੋਹਿ ਤ ਇਕ ਨ ਚਲੈ ਨਾਲਿ ॥
Arth

One might then abandon all these disciplines and instead apply siyanap (cleverness — the power of the intellect). Surely wisdom, scholarship, the company of learned ones — surely this will break through. To this, Guru Ji says: even if one possesses thousands and hundreds of thousands of siyanapaa — whether it is one's own mental sharpness, study of the scriptures, or learning absorbed from the company of scholars and wise people — because all of it is rooted in haumai, it stays on this side. Not even one of these clever strategies accompanies the soul to the realm of the True Reality.

Siyanap makes the intellect sharp. It produces great speakers and writers. But it does not dissolve the kooR dī pāl — the wall of falsehood, which is haumai. In fact it strengthens it. The very pride of "I am wise, I am learned, I am a scholar, I am a yogi" — that is haumai in its most sophisticated form. The True Form is agam agochhar — beyond the reach of the mind and intellect entirely.
ਕਿਵ ਸਚਿਆਰਾ ਹੋਈਐ ਕਿਵ ਕੂੜੈ ਤੁਟੈ ਪਾਲਿ ॥
Arth

Then how does one become sachiaar — one who has become truth itself, who has attained the Sat Saroop? And how is the kooR dī pāl — the wall of falsehood, the haumai — broken? The wall that stands between us and Him, creating the distance?

This is the turning point of the Pauri. Every door has been closed — ritual, silence, fasting, intellect. All have been shown to fail for the same reason: they are all driven by haumai, and haumai cannot dissolve itself. The question is genuine, not rhetorical. It is the seeker stripped of all their methods, asking with full sincerity.
ਹੁਕਮਿ ਰਜਾਈ ਚਲਣਾ ਨਾਨਕ ਲਿਖਿਆ ਨਾਲਿ ॥੧॥
Arth

O Nanak — by walking in hukam rajaa'ee: in alignment with the hukam (the Will, the cosmic law and order) of the One who is the master of that hukam and that razaa (sovereign Will) — one becomes sachiaar. And where is this hukam? Likhiaa naal — it is written alongside. Written within the very nature of the jeev (the living being). It is already there, inscribed in the srisht (nature/constitution) of every person.

Bhai Vir Singh explains: every human being naturally desires that those around them — friends, family, companions — be aligned with them, not against them. This longing for harmony is written into human nature itself. In the same way, the one who seeks the Sat Purakh (the True Being) must walk in alignment with Waheguru's hukam — not against it, not in resistance to it. This alignment — this ankulataa (going with, rather than against) — is the path to becoming sachiaar. It is not a new technique. It is a recognition of what is already written within.

Vyākhyā — Extended Commentary

The Wall is Haumai — Ego

The kooR dī pāl — the wall of falsehood — that stands between the seeker and the Sat Saroop (True Form of Akaal Purakh) is haumai. It is an inner wall, surrounding the innermost self, creating the distance from the True Form. No external act can reach it. In fact karam kaand (ritual religious acts) do not merely fail to remove haumai — they actively feed it.

ਕਰਮ ਕਰਤ ਬਦੇ ਅਹੰਮੇਵ ॥ "Through the performance of religious acts, ego only grows." — Kabir Ji (Gauri)

On Moun — Silence

Moun is a form of tap (austerity). It creates an outward stillness, but the inside keeps churning. The body is made of the three gun (qualities of nature — creation, sustenance, dissolution) and so its needs and drives do not fall silent just because the mouth does. The stream of thoughts and doubts, driven by haumai, does not stop. The antahkaran (the inner faculty of heart and mind) remains in agitation.

ਤਪੇ ਤਪੀਸਰ ਡੋਲੈ ਚੀਤ ॥ "Even the greatest masters of austerity — their minds still waver." — Kabir Ji (Gond)
ਬੋਲੈ ਨਾਹੀ ਹੋਇ ਬੈਠਾ ਮੋਨੀ ॥ ਅੰਤਰਿ ਕਲਪ ਭਵਾਈਐ ਜੋਨੀ ॥ "One sits silent, observing moun — yet inside, the mind keeps wandering through births." — M:5 (Prabhaati, Ashatpadi)

On Brat — Fasting

Trishnaa (craving) arises from haumai. As long as haumai remains, trishnaa remains, and the kooR dī pāl stays standing. The scriptural reward for fasting is worldly attainment — but the nature of trishnaa is that the more it is given, the more it demands. Trishnaa ends only through the abandonment of trishnaa itself — not through feeding it, even with spiritual rewards.

ਭੁਖਿਆ ਭੁਖ ਨ ਉਤਰੈ ਗਲੀ ਭੁਖ ਨ ਜਾਇ ॥ ਨਾਨਕ ਭੁਖਾ ਤਾ ਰਜੈ ਜਾ ਗੁਣ ਕਹਿ ਗੁਣੀ ਸਮਾਇ ॥ "The hungry one is not satisfied by talking about hunger — hunger does not leave through words. O Nanak, the hungry one is truly satisfied only when they sing the virtues of the Virtuous One and merge within Him." — Var Majh, M:2. The only true satiation is merger in the Source.
ਸਾਚੁ ਨਾਮੁ ਆਧਾਰੁ ਮੇਰਾ ਜਿਨਿ ਭੁਖਾ ਸਭਿ ਗਵਾਈਆ ॥ "The True Name is my very foundation — it is what has removed all my hungers." — Anand Sahib, M:3

On Siyanap — Cleverness and Intellect

Siyanap — the power of the intellect — whether it is one's own mental sharpness, the study of scriptures, or wisdom gained through the company of scholars — makes one a sharp thinker, a capable speaker, a learned writer. But it does not dissolve the kooR dī pāl. In fact, it strengthens haumai. The agam agochhar Sat Saroop — the True Form, beyond reach and beyond perception of the senses — lies entirely beyond the grasp of mind and intellect.

ਹਮ ਬਡ ਕਬਿ ਕੁਲੀਨ ਹਮ ਪੰਡਿਤ ਹਮ ਜੋਗੀ ਸੰਨਿਆਸੀ ॥ ਗਿਆਨੀ ਗੁਨੀ ਸੂਰ ਹਮ ਦਾਤੇ ਇਹ ਬੁਧਿ ਕਬਹਿ ਨ ਨਾਸੀ ॥ "I am a great poet, of noble lineage, a scholar, a yogi, a renunciant. I am the wise one, the virtuous one, the brave one, the generous one — this ego-intellect never perishes." — Ravidas Ji. The achievement list is itself the trap.
ਸਹਸ ਸਿਆਣਪ ਨਹ ਮਿਲੈ ਮੇਰੀ ਜਿੰਦੁੜੀਏ ॥ ਜਨ ਨਾਨਕ ਗੁਰਮੁਖਿ ਜਾਤਾ ਰਾਮ ॥ "Through thousands of clever strategies, O my soul, He is not found. Servant Nanak knows Him only as Gurmukh." — M:4, Bihagra
ਹਿਕਮਤਿ ਹੁਕਮਿ ਨ ਪਾਇਆ ਜਾਇ ॥ ਕਿਉ ਕਰਿ ਸਾਚਿ ਮਿਲਉ ਮੇਰੀ ਮਾਇ ॥ "Through schemes and clever strategies, He cannot be found. How then shall I meet the True One, O my mother?" — Dhana, M:1

The True Answer — Hukam

ਹੁਕਮਿ ਰਜਾਈ ਚਲਣਾ ਨਾਨਕ ਲਿਖਿਆ ਨਾਲਿ

Walk in hukam rajaa'ee — in alignment with the sovereign Will that governs all of existence. And where is this hukam? It is likhiaa naal — written alongside, inscribed within the very nature of every living being. It is not a new technique to acquire. It is a recognition of what is already there. The shift is from striving against the natural order to aligning with it — from haumai's resistance to the conscious, grateful walking in razaa (the Divine Will).

Key Terms — Pauri 1

ਸਚਿਆਰਾ Sachiaaraa — one who has become truth itself. Not merely someone who tells the truth, but one whose very being is aligned with the Sat Saroop (True Form). The destination of this Pauri.
ਕੂੜ ਦੀ ਪਾਲ KooR dī pāl — the wall of falsehood. This wall is identified as haumai. It stands around the innermost self and creates the distance between the individual and the True Form.
ਹਉਮੈ Haumai — ego; the deeply rooted sense of "I" as a separate self. The root cause behind the failure of all four methods. Not mere arrogance — a fundamental orientation of the self around itself rather than around Reality.
ਤ੍ਰਿਸ਼ਨਾ Trishnaa — craving; the inner hunger of desire. Arises from haumai. Cannot be satisfied by receiving more — it only grows with each satisfaction.
ਸਿਆਣਪ Siyanap — cleverness; the power of the intellect. Includes scholarship, philosophical reasoning, scriptural knowledge. All functions of the mind — and therefore unable to reach what lies beyond the mind.
ਹੁਕਮ Hukam — the Will; the cosmic order and law governing all of existence. Not an external command to obey with reluctance — it is written within the nature of every living being. Walking in hukam means aligning with what is already inscribed within you.
ਰਜਾਈ Razaa / Rajaa'ee — sovereign Will; the pleasure and Will of the Divine. Hukam rajaa'ee together means walking in joyful, grateful alignment with that Will — not reluctant compliance but willing harmony.
✦ Reflection Pointers
  • All four failed methods — ritual, silence, fasting, intellect — share one root cause: haumai is still driving them. Where in your own life do you see effort that feels spiritual but is still ego-driven?
  • Bhai Vir Singh says hukam is already likhiaa naal — written within you. What does it mean to stop searching for something that was never absent?
  • The shift from haumai to hukam is described as ankulataa — going with rather than against. What would it feel like to stop resisting the natural order of your life?
Learn With Nanak — Amardeep Singh & Vininder Kaur
This interpretation is from LearnWithNanak.com by Amardeep Singh and Vininder Kaur. It is their reading — accessible, philosophical, and grounded in lived experience. It is presented here as one lens among several, not as the definitive interpretation.
The Context — Pauri 1 Thoughts come and go, emotions rise and fall. The mind can get caught in its own entanglements of illusion, causing doubt and confusion. Unrestrained desires create aspirations that never entirely settle. Guru Nanak does not disregard these tendencies — he acknowledges them as part of being human, and reassures that they can be transcended through critical reflection and integrity.
ਸੋਚੈ ਸੋਚਿ ਨ ਹੋਵਈ ਜੇ ਸੋਚੀ ਲਖ ਵਾਰ ॥
Learn With Nanak

The mind, devoid of a permanent entity, identifies itself with multiple fleeting thoughts which emerge from external stimulants and internal dissatisfaction. This commotion does not allow the mind to transcend to its eternal, pristine state of stillness.

ਚੁਪੈ ਚੁਪ ਨ ਹੋਵਈ ਜੇ ਲਾਇ ਰਹਾ ਲਿਵ ਤਾਰ ॥
Learn With Nanak

Silence refers to the absence of sound. In a spiritual context, it is an internal dimension rather than an external one. An individual can appear externally quiet but can be fraught with internal noise despite being in silent meditation.

ਭੁਖਿਆ ਭੁਖ ਨ ਉਤਰੀ ਜੇ ਬੰਨਾ ਪੁਰੀਆ ਭਾਰ ॥
Learn With Nanak

Worldly illusions arouse desires and insecurities, creating a delusion that material wealth is the path to overall well-being. This misconception leads to a hunger to accumulate resources which never satiates.

ਸਹਸ ਸਿਆਣਪਾ ਲਖ ਹੋਹਿ ਤ ਇਕ ਨ ਚਲੈ ਨਾਲਿ ॥
Learn With Nanak

Knowledge refers to the information gathered from various sources that can become a reason for the intellectual ego and a barrier to moving towards gaining awareness of the unseen and unknown.

ਕਿਵ ਸਚਿਆਰਾ ਹੋਈਐ ਕਿਵ ਕੂੜੈ ਤੁਟੈ ਪਾਲਿ ॥
Learn With Nanak

How can the mind recognize its pristine innate being? How can the barrage of misconceptions be shattered?

ਹੁਕਮਿ ਰਜਾਈ ਚਲਣਾ ਨਾਨਕ ਲਿਖਿਆ ਨਾਲਿ ॥੧॥
Learn With Nanak

According to Nanak, accepting the path of complete harmony with the laws of nature is the ultimate principle of creating a fulfilling self-providence. Those who do not consider themselves separate from the laws of nature rise above ritualistic practices and societal conditioning. They recognize the connection between the cosmic laws and the inherent inner being. Ultimately they attain the truth — which helps them transcend the unreal state of disharmony to the innate quietude of being One with nature.
