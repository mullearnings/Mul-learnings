Why Grammar Matters Here

Prof. Sahib Singh spent a decade — from 1920 onward — establishing something no one had systematically proven before: that the language of Sri Guru Granth Sahib follows its own specific grammatical rules, just as every language of every land and era follows specific grammar.

The Japji Sahib Steek was his second commentary. His warning is direct: the spellings of Old Punjabi differ considerably from modern Punjabi. The grammar lives inside those spellings. Miss the spellings, miss the grammar, miss the meaning.

He writes: "If readers do not develop the habit of recognizing and identifying the word-spellings of Gurbani, my hard work will not be of full benefit to them."

Example 1 — ਸੁਣਿਐ vs ਸੁਣੀਐ
ਸੁਣਿਐ / ਸੁਣੀਐ Same root, different grammatical form — different meaning
ਗਾਵੀਐ ਸੁਣੀਐ ਮਨਿ ਰਖੀਐ ਭਾਉ ॥
Pauri 5
"Sing, and listen, and keep love enshrined in the mind."
ਸੁਣੀਐ = impersonal — "one should listen / let us listen"
ਸੁਣਿਐ ਸਤੁ ਸੰਤੋਖੁ ਗਿਆਨੁ ॥
Pauri 10
"By listening — truth, contentment, and wisdom."
ਸੁਣਿਐ = absolutive — "by the act of listening / having listened"
One letter difference in spelling signals a completely different grammatical function — and a different meaning. Miss it and you've misread the verse.
Example 2 — ਭਗਤ vs ਭਗਤਿ
ਭਗਤ / ਭਗਤਿ Person vs practice — noun vs abstract noun
ਅਸੰਖ ਭਗਤ ਗੁਣ ਗਿਆਨ ਵੀਚਾਰੁ ॥
Pauri 17
"Countless devotees contemplate virtues and wisdom."
ਭਗਤ = a devotee — the person
ਵਿਣੁ ਗੁਣ ਕੀਤੇ ਭਗਤਿ ਨ ਹੋਇ ॥
Pauri 21
"Without cultivating virtues, devotion does not happen."
ਭਗਤਿ = devotion — the practice itself
Read ਭਗਤਿ as ਭਗਤ and you've turned "devotion" into "a devotee" — the entire sentence changes meaning.
Example 3 — ਤੇ vs ਤੈ
ਤੇ / ਤੈ Modern "and" vs classical "from" — easily confused
ਤਿਸੁ ਤੇ ਹੋਏ ਲਖ ਦਰੀਆਉ ॥
Pauri 19
"From That One, hundreds of thousands of rivers flow."
ਤੇ here = "from" — not "and"
ਆਖਹਿ ਗੋਪੀ ਤੈ ਗੋਵਿੰਦ ॥
Pauri 26
"The gopis speak — and Govind."
ਤੈ = ਅਤੇ = "and" in classical Gurbani — always appears this way in SGGS
The rule: In modern Punjabi, ਤੇ/ਅਤੇ = "and." In Gurbani, ਤੈ = "and" while ਤੇ = "from." They look similar. They mean opposite things.
Why Start with a Biography?

Prof. Sahib Singh opens with a brief life summary because understanding who composed Japji Sahib is inseparable from understanding what Japji Sahib is. This was not composed by a scholar in a library. It emerged from a life of radical, direct engagement with the full spectrum of human spiritual seeking.

Born 1469 CE
Sammat 1526
Talwandi
Birth & Early Life
Father: Baba Kalu Ji, village accountant. One elder sister: Bibi Nanaki Ji, five years older. At age 7, sent to school. At age 9, refused the sacred thread (janeu) ceremony — already rejecting ritual without understanding. At 16, betrothed to the daughter of Baba Mula Ji of Batala. Married at 18.
1504 CE
Sultanpur
Vein River
Departure & The Vein River
Moved to Sultanpur at invitation of brother-in-law Bhai Jai Ram Ji. Took Bhai Mardana along. Worked as store-keeper for Nawab Daulat Khan. On 21 Bhadon 1564 (Sammat), at the Vein river, he entered deep contemplation. Seeing the world in its suffering, he decided he must leave comfort behind and show the world the correct path.
1507–1515 CE
First Udaasi
8 years
First Journey — Hindu Tirthas (East & South)
Visited every major Hindu pilgrimage site. At each tirtha, the message was the same: outer pilgrimage without inner transformation is meaningless. Places visited: Lahore, Haridwar, Ayodhya, Prayag, Varanasi, Gaya, Patna, Assam, Jagannath Puri, Rameswaram, Sangladip (Sri Lanka), Somnath, Dwarka, Mathura, Vrindavan, Delhi, Panipat, Kurukshetra.
1517–1518 CE
Second Udaasi
1 year
Second Journey — Uttarkhand, Siddh Yogis
Traveled to the mountain retreats of the Siddh Yogis in Uttarkhand. Engaged them in the famous dialogues that would later become Siddh Gosht. Returned to Kartarpur.
1518–1521 CE
Third Udaasi
3 years
Third Journey — West (Mecca, Baghdad)
Traveled west wearing blue robes. Pakpattan — received Baba Farid Ji's bani. Mecca — Jion Haji tried to drag him by the feet. Baghdad — sang kirtan in a city that considered music forbidden. Balkh, Kabul, Peshawar. Returned via Eminabad (1521) at the time of Babur's invasion.
1521–1539 CE
Kartarpur
18 final years
The Kartarpur Years & Passing
Settled permanently at Kartarpur. Composed Asa di Var, Japji Sahib, Oankaar, Siddh Gosht. Bhai Mardana passed away. Baba Lehna (future Guru Angad) came in 1532. Named Guru Angad as successor on 2 September 1539. Passed into the eternal light on 7 September 1539. Total lifespan: 70 years, 4 months, approximately 19 days.
The Architecture of Japji

Prof. Sahib Singh groups the 38 Pauris into seven thematic clusters. Japji is not 38 random verses — it has a building structure, each cluster deepening what came before. The central question the entire composition answers: how does the soul close its distance from the Divine?

Pauris 1–3 · The Problem & The Law
The distance between the soul and the Divine is caused by Maya — falsehood, illusion. The only way to close that distance is to walk in Hukam — the Divine Will, the cosmic law. This principle has operated since creation began. Billions have tried to estimate this Will; none have fully grasped it. Yet within it, infinite gifts flow to infinite beings.
Pauris 4–7 · What Actually Works
Charity, ritual offerings, pilgrimage bathing, pranayam — none of these alone close the distance. The only language in which one can speak to the Divine is Prem — Love. The person who rises at Amrit Vela and connects through remembrance receives the robe of Love. Devotion is not earned. It is received as grace.
Pauris 8–11 · What Simran Does
As awareness connects with Naam, the mind expands. Previous vices drop away. The knowledge locked inside scripture opens spontaneously. The ocean of the world — where a lost soul thrashes blindly — becomes navigable. The mind enters Sahaj: effortless equilibrium.
Pauris 12–15 · The Transformation Deepens
The soul rises above Maya's influence. The Divine is seen everywhere in creation. Fear of death dissolves. The connection with Simran becomes so deep that no obstacle can uproot it. All bonds that created separation from the Divine break.
Pauris 16–19 · The Blessed & The Infinite
The fortunate ones are those who have made the Guru's path their life's purpose. But even they cannot find the limits of creation. No human language contains a word capable of counting what the Divine has made. Language itself is a gift — given for the purpose of praise.
Pauris 20–27 · Cleansing & Incomprehensibility
Maya dirties the mind. Simran is the only cleanser. The Divine's greatness is such that the more you describe it, the greater it appears. No scholar — past, present, or future — can complete the account of what the Divine is.
Pauris 28–33 · Simran as the Path
Simran — devotional remembrance — is the life's purpose. Not yogic garments, not external markers. Simran builds contentment, righteous effort, awareness of death, love for creation, recognition of the Divine in all things.
Pauris 34–38 · Grace & The Final Teaching
Those upon whom grace falls first understand: they came to this earth with a specific duty. The final teaching: this world is a stage. Those who played only the game of Maya departed separated. Those who played the game of Simran — they succeeded.
Prof. Sahib Singh's Own Questions

At the end of his introduction, Prof. Sahib Singh poses and answers the fundamental questions about Japji Sahib — why it is called what it is, why it opens the Guru Granth Sahib, and why it must be recited daily.

How does the soul close its distance from the Divine?
By walking in Hukam — the Divine Will. Just as a son who continuously follows his father's will has no distance from his father, the soul that aligns with the Divine Will closes the gap. What does not work: charity, pilgrimage bathing, pranayam, life extension. None of these close the distance.
Pauris 1–7
How does one walk in Hukam?
Through Simran — devotional remembrance and praise. As one walks the Guru's path and engages in Simran, the Divine Will gradually begins to feel sweet rather than burdensome. The awareness must become so deeply absorbed in praise that the mind settles into the Divine and no longer wants to pull away.
Pauris 8–15
What is the result of this practice?
Not the ability to measure or comprehend the infinite creation — that will never happen. The result is simpler and more profound: the habit of resting in the Divine Will becomes permanent. The soul is no longer fighting reality.
Pauris 16–27
Why is this composition called "Jap"?
The entire composition has one argument: none of the conventional religious paths can reunite the separated soul with the Divine. Only one thing can — Simran, devotional remembrance. That is why the composition is named Jap. The word Jap means exactly this: Simran, Bandagi, Bhajan — remembrance, devotion, praise.
Why does it open the Guru Granth Sahib?
The central foundation of Sikh Dharam is Simran — the foundational pillar upon which the entire building of Dharam is constructed. The composition that most fully expresses this theme is Japji. So Guru Arjan Sahib placed it at the very beginning of the Guru Granth Sahib.
Why recite it every day?
As one remembers the Divine, love for the Divine grows. As love grows, the Divine Will begins to feel sweet. As the Will feels sweet, one walks in alignment naturally. Daily recitation is not ritual — it is the practice of building and maintaining this living love. Distance from the Divine is caused by forgetting. Remembrance is the only remedy.
