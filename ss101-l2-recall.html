Part 1 · Entering the bhakti world
What three features define the bhakti movement?
A direct, individual connection to the Divine; the removal of intermediaries (pandit, sheikh/qazi class); and communal gatherings (sangat/satsang) — not solitary withdrawal.

What is the "founder myth," and how is it challenged?
The view (esp. McLeod) that Guru Nanak sits within the existing bhakti stream, with a distinct Sikh identity emerging only later (Khalsa). It's challenged by early sources — Bhai Gurdas's "apnā panth nirālā" and the Persian Dabistan — showing a distinct community forming early.
Tap to reveal
In the B40 Janam Sakhi, how is the Kabir–Nanak relationship framed?
Reversed: Kabir becomes the Sikh/disciple of Guru Nanak, praising him as the giver of salvation and diminishing even Ramananda (Kabir's own remembered guru).

Why does the question of the Kabir–Nanak meeting matter so much?
It frames authority — was it equality, succession, discipleship, or correction? Underneath: did Guru Nanak inherit Kabir's path, or stand on his own authority?

Part 2 · Kartarpur & honest work
What does Kartarpur represent?
Settled, ethical, communal life — transformed participation in the world rather than wandering withdrawal. The path embodied in how one lives, works, and shares.
Tap to reveal
What is the ideal at the heart of the GGS 1245 shabad?
The one who works hard for what he eats and gives some to others knows the path — joining labour, honesty, sustenance, and generosity, and rejecting both exploitative religiosity and escapist renunciation.
Tap to reveal
What is the threefold pattern of Sikh life?
Nām Japo · Kirat Karo · Vand Chhako — recite the Divine Name, earn an honest livelihood, and share with others.
Tap to reveal
How is the langar "the teaching enacted"?
At Kartarpur, Guru Nanak farms the land and feeds the community from that labour — remembrance, ethical work, and generosity joined in one practice, continued by the later Gurus.

Part 3 · One light, many bodies
What does "one light, many bodies" mean?
Succession transfers the same jyot (light), mission, and authority — not just leadership. As Ramkali ki Vār puts it, "the King merely changed his body."
Tap to reveal
What is "Mahalla," and what does it teach?
The title for the Guru authors in scripture (from Arabic hulul, "descent"): Guru Nanak = Mahalla Pehla, Guru Angad = Duja, and so on. The headings themselves teach the doctrine of succession.
Tap to reveal
How do the Gurus sign their verses — and why?
They all sign "Nanak" (not Angad, Amar Das, etc.), placing their writing within a single current of Guruship — Nanak's light speaking through successive forms.
Tap to reveal
Which sources — inside and outside — attest the "one light" idea?
Inside: Guru Gobind Singh's Bachittra Nāṭak. Outside: the Persian Dabistan (1645), describing Nanak passing into Angad, Amar Das, Ram Das, Arjan.

Part 4 · The Gurus & the Panth
Who was Lehna, and what changed him?
A devotee of the goddess Durga. Passing through Kartarpur, he heard Guru Nanak's praise and stopped; in the narrative he sees Durga herself doing seva there — recognising Guru Nanak's supremacy. He served, was tested, and became Guru Angad.
Tap to reveal
What did Guru Angad formalise, and why does it matter?
Gurmukhi. A distinct script helps create and sustain a distinct community — marking out a Sikh mode of preserving and transmitting revelation, apart from Devanagari and Persian/Arabic.
Tap to reveal
Why is Mata Khivi significant?
She is named in Guru Granth Sahib (GGS 967) for the langar — showing the kitchen was a central, honoured institution, which she actively oversaw and nourished.
Tap to reveal
How was Bibi Amaro the "bridge" between the 2nd and 3rd Gurus?
She had memorised Japji, Dakhani Oankar, and Siddh Gosht; hearing her sing Gurbani at dawn, Guru Amar Das was pierced by the Shabad and drawn to its source — and so to Guru Angad.
Tap to reveal
What did Guru Amar Das build and organise?
Goindval and its baoli (step well); the Vaisakhi & Diwali gatherings; the masand system; 900+ verses; and the Goindwal Pothis.
Tap to reveal
What were the masands originally — and how did the word turn negative?
Originally district spiritual guides and administrators, an extension of the Guru's arm (from masnad, "seat"). Only later, when the office became hereditary, did some grow corrupt — giving the word its negative charge.
Tap to reveal
Why do the Goindwal Pothis matter?
They are the oldest extant manuscript compilation and show early Gurmukhi (larivaar, undeveloped vowel signs) — a reminder that scripture passed through material, scribal, and linguistic histories.
Tap to reveal
Who was Bibi Bhani, and what boon did she ask for?
Daughter of Guru Amar Das (the throne-leg seva story). She asked that Guruship continue through her lineage — making her the matriarch of the Sodhi line (wife of Guru Ram Das, mother of Guru Arjan, on to Guru Gobind Singh).
Tap to reveal
What broader point do Mata Khivi, Bibi Amaro & Bibi Bhani make together?
Sikh women were formative, not peripheral — nourishing langar, transmitting Gurbani, drawing future Gurus into the Guru's presence, and standing at the centre of the lineage.

What did Guru Ram Das establish?
The city of Ramdaspur (later Amritsar), on land favoured by Akbar; 679 verses, including the Lāvāṅ.

Part 5 · Assembling the Adi Granth
Why did Guru Arjan compile the Adi Granth?
To make an authoritative stamp on legitimate Gurbani — guarding against forgeries (e.g. the Pran Sangli passed off as Nanak's), sealed with the Mundavani. Also as a sign of community authority (Pashaura Singh; Akbar's 1598 visit).
Tap to reveal
Besides the Gurus, whose voices are in the Adi Granth?
The Bhagats — Kabir, Ravidas, Sheikh Farid, Namdev, Jaidev, Pipa, Trilochan, Beni, and others — plus the Bhatt Bani, Balwand, and even Bhai Mardana.
Tap to reveal
How is devotional authority given to the Adi Granth once compiled?
It is placed on the takht in Harimandir Sahib; the sangat bows to the scripture, not to Guru Arjan, who sleeps on the floor beside it while it rests higher.
Tap to reveal
The three broad sections of the Adi Granth?
Liturgical prayers (Japji, Sodar, So Purakh, Sohila) → 31 raags (the bulk, Srī Rāg to Jaijaivantī) → epilogue (saloks, Mundāvaṇi, Rāgamālā).
Tap to reveal
Why does raag matter for interpretation?
Gurbani is meant to be sung: the raag carries the ras/mood and works on the mind, drawing it toward the inward self. The musical form is part of the meaning — the medium is part of the message.
Tap to reveal
The three kāṇḍ in the traditional (sampradāyik) reading?
Karam (action/virtue) · Upāsanā / Bhakti (devotion) · Gyān (wisdom) — interwoven throughout Gurbani, not a rigid ladder.
Tap to reveal
In the four-poets story, why is Kahna's true non-dual verse excluded?
Because truth without bhakti is dangerous — in Kalyug the ego seizes "I am that." Wisdom must be joined to devotion (the image: ghee needs sweetness to be digested).
Tap to reveal
Why is Chajju's verse rejected — and what does that reveal?
It speaks against women. The Guru established a grihasthi (householder) panth that respects women — so anti-women thought is excluded. (Grounded already in Guru Nanak's own defence of women.)
Tap to reveal
"One line, more than one meaning" — give the example.
"Visar na dātār" reads devotionally ("may I not forget the One who gives to all") and toward gyan ("may I not forget that I am that reality which gives to all").
Tap to reveal
Dualistic vs non-dualistic ways of reading Gurbani?
Dualistic isolates words for one fixed meaning (Singh Sabha / rationalist). Non-dualistic is holistic — context, the utanka, performance, raag, recitation — keeping Gurbani "new every morning."
